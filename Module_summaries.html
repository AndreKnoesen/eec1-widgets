ECE Emerge: Module Summaries (Modules 1–7)

Module 1: Foundations and Circuit Language

Module 1 introduces the fundamental language of Electrical and Computer Engineering, focusing on the concepts of charge, current, and electric potential. You will begin to see the op-amp as an abstraction—a "black box" that hides internal complexity.

  • Electric Potential & Conventional Current: Understanding how energy is moved through a circuit.
  • Hands-On: You will be introduced to the ADALM2000 (M2K) and complete the essential task of soldering your adaptor board.

Module 2: DC Circuits and the Voltage Divider

This module establishes the core laws used to analyze any circuit, moving from individual components to networked systems.

  • KCL & KVL: Mastering Kirchhoff’s Current and Voltage Laws to track charge and energy conservation.
  • The Voltage Divider: Learning the "cornerstone" circuit that appears in almost every engineering application.
  • Laboratory: You will perform DC voltage measurements and verify series/parallel relationships on your breadboard.

Module 3: Signals in Time and Frequency

Module 3 shifts the focus from constant DC values to time-varying signals, introducing the duality between time and frequency domains.

  • Fourier Series Synthesis: Understanding how complex periodic waves are built from simple sine waves.
  • Frequency Domain: Using the M2K to visualize signals beyond the standard oscilloscope view.

Module 4: Circuits That Remember (RC and RL)

This module introduces memory elements—capacitors and inductors—and analyzes how they respond to sudden changes in a circuit.

  • Time Constants: Defining how quickly a circuit charges or discharges.
  • MATLAB Integration: Using computational tools to extract time constants from physical laboratory data.

Module 5: Phasors, Impedance, and Filters

Module 5 acts as a bridge, unifying signals and circuits to analyze systems driven by oscillating AC signals.

  • Phasors & Complex Numbers: Converting difficult differential equations into straightforward algebra.
  • Filters: Understanding how RC circuits can be used as Low-Pass or High-Pass filters to "tune" signals.

Module 6(a): Op-Amp Fundamentals

Week 6 introduces the operational amplifier (op-amp), exploring the high-gain active device and the concept of negative feedback.

  • The Golden Rules: Applying the "Virtual Short" and "Virtual Open" concepts to analyze ideal op-amp circuits.
  • Voltage Follower: Proving how a buffer isolates circuit stages and prevents loading effects.
  • Slew Rate: Measuring the real-world physical speed limits of an op-amp's output.

Module 6(b): Applied Op-Amps and Level Shifting

Week 7 puts your op-amp knowledge to work by building the family of amplifiers required for the final project.

  • Inverting & Non-Inverting Amplifiers: Designing circuits where gain is precisely set by external resistors.
  • Summing Amplifiers: Learning to "mix" signals and shift DC levels—a key requirement for the Scale Capstone project.
  • Saturation: Observing what happens when signals exceed the power supply rails.

Module 7: Instrumentation Amplifiers and Capstone Launch

Module 7 transitions from structured lab exercises to an open-ended engineering design project: a high-resolution digital scale.

  • The Signal in the Difference: Using the Wheatstone bridge to convert mechanical force into tiny differential voltages.
  • Instrumentation Amplifiers: Utilizing the INA125 to provide high input impedance and superior common-mode rejection for precise sensor measurement.
  • Signal Chain Optimization: Designing level shifters to fill the 5V ADC input range, maximizing your scale's resolution to weigh a single jelly bean.
  • Engineering Bring-Up: Executing your team’s custom breadboard bring-up plan to verify the integrated signal chain.
